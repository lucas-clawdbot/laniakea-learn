Let's start with the basics. Sky Ecosystem powers USDS — a large decentralized, yield-generating stablecoin. As of late 2025, we're talking about roughly ten billion dollars in USDS supply, over four hundred million in annualized protocol revenue, and about a hundred and sixty-eight million in annualized profit. This thing grew around eighty-six percent in 2025, outpacing the overall stablecoin market which grew about fifty percent. So it's not just big — it's growing faster than the category.

Sky evolved from MakerDAO, which launched back in 2017 with DAI. Eight years of continuous operation through multiple market cycles — the 2018 bear market, the March 2020 crash where ETH dropped over fifty percent in a single day, the 2022 crypto winter. That track record matters. DAI still works and is convertible one-to-one with USDS.

The revenue model is straightforward. Sky earns a base rate on deployed capital, pays a savings rate to sUSDS holders, and keeps the spread. Revenue comes from collateralized lending, digital asset-backed lending, U.S. Treasuries, private credit like CLO allocations, and delta-neutral strategies. Diversification means they're not dependent on any single market regime, which has historically dampened sensitivity to crypto price cycles.

Now let's talk tokens. There are three to know.

USDS is the stablecoin — pegged one-to-one to the dollar, overcollateralized by crypto assets, Treasuries, and credit positions, freely transferable, available on Ethereum mainnet plus multiple L2s and L1s via SkyLink. Unlike payment stablecoins that fall under frameworks like the GENIUS Act, USDS is specifically designed for capital formation and yield generation. There are about three hundred billion dollars in stablecoins sitting idle without earning yield right now. That's the market opportunity.

sUSDS is the savings token. It's an ERC-4626 vault. You deposit USDS, get sUSDS back, and the exchange rate increases continuously at the Sky Savings Rate. No lockups, freely redeemable at any time. It even earns yield on other chains through SkyLink. Simple and elegant.

SKY is the governance token. Fixed supply of 23.46 billion, emissions permanently disabled. The protocol buys back SKY on the open market using profits. Since February 2025, over ninety-two million dollars in buybacks — that's over six percent of total supply purchased. SKY holders get three things: governance participation over all protocol parameters, programmatic profit allocation through those buybacks, and collateral utility — meaning you can use staked SKY as collateral for borrowing USDS while still earning staking rewards. That last one is especially clever because you effectively lower your borrowing costs without sacrificing governance participation or yield.

Roughly seventy-five to a hundred percent of protocol profits flow to SKY stakers through the buyback mechanism. There's also a deflationary mechanic: about fourteen percent of the original MKR supply was never claimed after the migration, and the SKY backing that unclaimed MKR gets burned one percent per quarter over about twenty-four years.

Protocol revenue flows through a five-step waterfall called the Treasury Management Function. Step one takes care of security and maintenance — twenty-one percent during the genesis period, dropping to four to ten percent post-genesis. Step two fills the aggregate backstop capital — a solvency buffer targeting one-and-a-half percent of total supply, filling dynamically based on how far it is from target. Step three funds the Fortification Conserver for legal defense and resilience. Step four powers the Smart Burn Engine — those SKY buybacks. Step five distributes everything remaining to staking rewards. Steps three and four scale up with a hyperbolic net revenue ratio function, so as the protocol earns more, proportionally more goes to burn and legal infrastructure.

Now here's where Laniakea really gets interesting — the Agent Network. This is the capital allocation layer sitting between Sky Protocol and actual investments. The flow goes: Sky Protocol, to Synomic Agents called Primes, to investment wrappers called Halos, to end assets.

Primes are the primary capital deployers. Think of them as governance-approved operators that receive capital from Sky Protocol and deploy it into strategies. Each Prime has its own treasury, its own governance token, and a specialized focus. They bear the business risk, providing their own capital as first-loss protection on assets they deploy. This separates risk-taking from monetary policy.

Right now there are three operational Star Primes. Spark handles DeFi lending including SparkLend and the Spark Liquidity Layer across six chains. Grove does private credit and CLO allocations. Keel handles ecosystem expansion including Solana deployment and USDS adoption. There's also an Institutional Prime called Obex for incubation. Two more Star Prime slots — Star4 and Star5 — are reserved for future expansion.

The beautiful competitive dynamic is that Primes compete for capital allocation based on performance. Better risk-adjusted returns means more capital. This creates market pressure for higher yields, deeper risk specialization, faster innovation, and greater efficiency through shared infrastructure.

Halos are investment product wrappers created by Primes. Each Halo wraps a specific strategy with standardized interfaces. There are three main types, and this is important to understand.

Portfolio Halos use LCTS — the Liquidity Constrained Token Standard. It's a queue-based system for capacity-constrained investments. You subscribe assets to a queue, and at daily settlement at sixteen hundred UTC, deposits convert to Halo shares at the current exchange rate. If capacity is constrained, conversion may span multiple settlement cycles. The queue locks during the processing window from thirteen hundred to sixteen hundred UTC for deterministic calculations and fair allocation. This is the natural fit for most real-world investments where you can't just dump unlimited capital in.

Term Halos use NFATs — Non-Fungible Allocation Tokens. These represent individual bespoke deals with negotiated terms within defined buyboxes, meaning acceptable parameter ranges. Each position can have different duration, size, and yield. Think six-month, twelve-month, and eighteen-month positions with different yields, all under the same legal framework. NFATs are transferable, can be used as collateral, and can even be wrapped in fungible ERC-20 tokens for secondary markets.

Trading Halos run AMM smart contracts using predeposited USDS to provide instant liquidity for RWA tokens. Users sell tokenized real-world assets for instant USDS at a spread, and the Trading Halo redeems with the underlying issuer over the normal settlement cycle. This turns real-world asset liquidity from days-long redemption into instant settlement.

There are also Special Halos for identity networks handling KYC, Exchange Halos running orderbooks and matching engines, and Staking Halos for Growth Staking. Plus Core Halos that wrap legacy positions like Morpho vaults, Aave pools, and SparkLend under the same risk framework.

Speaking of Growth Staking — this is a clever incentive mechanism. SKY stakers must also hold growth assets like Agent tokens, risk capital tokens, or sSGA to unlock staking rewards. The more directly an asset contributes to innovation, the less you need. Agent tokens like Prime tokens need just 0.4x the value of your staked SKY. Junior risk capital needs 0.6x. Senior risk capital needs 1.0x. And passive sSGA needs 2.0x. This creates a powerful pull: same capital invested in a Prime token gives you five times the staking factor efficiency compared to holding sSGA. It structurally pushes capital from passive savings into the active innovation layer, and it eliminates mercenary staking because holding SKY alone earns you nothing.

Now the risk framework. This is genuinely sophisticated — it's inspired by Basel III banking regulation but adapted for decentralized operation. The core insight is that capital requirements should reflect the maximum loss that could actually be forced to realize, not just mark-to-market volatility.

There's a two-tier capital structure. Junior Risk Capital, or JRC, absorbs losses first in exchange for higher returns. Senior Risk Capital is protected by the JRC buffer and gets lower but safer returns. Each Prime provides its own JRC from its treasury — direct skin in the game.

The loss absorption waterfall is deep. At the Prime level: first, the Prime's own capital absorbs the first ten percent of JRC losses. Then all junior capital shares in. Then the Prime's governance token gets diluted through inflation — and this can cover losses essentially to infinity before touching senior capital. At the system level: senior risk capital absorbs next. Then SKY gets diluted. Nuclear options include genesis capital haircuts and USDS peg adjustment, which should never be reached.

Duration matching is where it gets really clever. Sky tracks how long USDS holders keep their positions using a Lindy model — the longer you've held, the longer you're likely to keep holding. This gives you liability duration data. Assets are then matched using Duration Buckets: if an asset takes a long time to liquidate, measured by something called Stressed Pull-to-Par time, it needs backing from correspondingly long-duration liabilities. Assets whose duration matches available liability capacity only need capital for fundamental risks like credit default. Unmatched assets need additional capital covering potential mark-to-market losses from credit spread widening. And all fixed-rate exposure must be hedged or carry extra capital.

There's also a risk capital ingression system — not all external capital counts at face value. It's discounted based on whether the provider is synomic, how long capital is committed, and the Prime's existing composition. A Prime token market cap constraint further limits total leverage. And concentration limits prevent any single correlated risk type from getting too large — anything exceeding a governance-defined category cap requires one hundred percent capitalization.

Let's talk about the smart contract architecture. Every layer uses the same building block: the PAU — Parallelized Allocation Unit. Three components: a Controller as the entry point enforcing rate limits, an ALMProxy that holds custody of funds and executes calls, and RateLimits for linear replenishment rate limits with configurable maximums and slopes. Same contracts everywhere — Generator, Prime, Halo, Foreign — just different configuration. This means no new code for new layers, factories can stamp out identical PAUs, and adding a layer just means deploying more instances.

There are four layers. The Generator layer interfaces with the stablecoin contract and deploys capital via ERC-4626 vaults. The Prime layer receives from Generators and deploys to Halos or bridges to other chains. The Halo layer deploys to RWA strategies and custodians. And the Foreign layer runs identical infrastructure on alt-chains, receiving bridged assets directly.

The Diamond PAU pattern uses EIP-2535 diamond proxy architecture — modular and upgradeable. You can add new action facets without full redeployment. Rate limits are the universal control mechanism. Every single capital flow is rate-limited. And here's a key invariant: losses are reflected instantly via exchange rate haircuts, while gains accrue gradually over time.

Now, autonomous operations. Laniakea introduces beacons classified on two axes: power and authority. Low Power, Low Authority beacons handle reporting. Low Power, High Authority beacons execute deterministic rules. High Power, Low Authority beacons do trading. And High Power, High Authority beacons are the Sentinels — AI-capable formations.

Sentinel formations are fascinating. They don't operate as single agents — they're coordinated systems with three components. The Baseline Sentinel is the public autopilot. It's the only entity holding keys to the execution engine. It runs public, auditable code and executes the base strategy. The Stream Sentinel handles data ingestion and signal generation, allowing proprietary intelligence to influence execution without exposing strategies or holding keys. The alpha stays private. And Warden Sentinels provide independent safety oversight — they can halt operations when things go wrong, without being compromised by the same failure modes.

If the Stream disconnects, the Baseline automatically reverts to its base strategy. It continuously simulates "what would we have earned with just the base strategy" for performance comparison. This data plane, control plane, safety plane separation is elegant engineering.

A sixth thing worth knowing: Spark Savings Vault Intents. This is a new system just documented in the spark-savings-intents repository that solves a real operational problem. When a user wants to withdraw a very large amount from a Spark Savings Vault — think millions of USDC — the vault might not have enough idle liquidity sitting around to process the redemption immediately. A direct on-chain redeem call would just revert, leaving them stuck.

The solution is an intent-based withdrawal mechanism. Instead of calling redeem directly, users submit a withdrawal intent specifying how many shares they want to redeem, who should receive the assets, and a deadline. The ALM Planner — an off-chain relayer system — monitors these intents, orchestrates the necessary liquidity by unwinding positions and moving assets into the vault, and then calls fulfill to atomically complete the redemption. The intent contract never holds any shares or assets itself. It's a pure coordination layer.

Three actors: the user who holds vault shares, the SavingsVaultIntents contract that stores requests and executes fulfillments, and the ALM Planner relayer that monitors events and orchestrates capital. A user can have one active request per vault at a time, can overwrite or cancel requests, and fulfillment is all-or-nothing — no partial fills. There's a clever race condition protection: the fulfill function takes a requestId parameter and validates it matches the stored request, preventing stale planner state from executing against a modified request.

Security posture is solid. Even a malicious relayer can't cause fund loss — worst they can do is not fulfill or let requests expire. There's a minimum intent size threshold to prevent spam. And the contract is designed specifically for Spark Vault v2. This is the plumbing that makes large institutional withdrawals work cleanly in the Laniakea context.
