Now here's where Laniakea really gets interesting — the Agent Network. This is the capital allocation layer sitting between Sky Protocol and actual investments. The flow goes: Sky Protocol, to Synomic Agents called Primes, to investment wrappers called Halos, to end assets.  Primes are the primary capital deployers. Think of them as governance-approved operators that receive capital from Sky Protocol and deploy it into strategies. Each Prime has its own treasury, its own governance token, and a specialized focus. They bear the business risk, providing their own capital as first-loss protection on assets they deploy. This separates risk-taking from monetary policy.  Right now there are three operational Star Primes. Spark handles DeFi lending including SparkLend and the Spark Liquidity Layer across six chains. Grove does private credit and CLO allocations. Keel handles ecosystem expansion including Solana deployment and USDS adoption. There's also an Institutional Prime called Obex for incubation. Two more Star Prime slots — Star4 and Star5 — are reserved for future expansion.  The beautiful competitive dynamic is that Primes compete for capital allocation based on performance. Better risk-adjusted returns means more capital. This creates market pressure for higher yields, deeper risk specialization, faster innovation, and greater efficiency through shared infrastructure.  Halos are investment product wrappers created by Primes. Each Halo wraps a specific strategy with standardized interfaces. There are three main types, and this is important to understand.  Portfolio Halos use LCTS — the Liquidity Constrained Token Standard. It's a queue-based system for capacity-constrained investments. You subscribe assets to a queue, and at daily settlement at sixteen hundred UTC, deposits convert to Halo shares at the current exchange rate. If capacity is constrained, conversion may span multiple settlement cycles. The queue locks during the processing window from thirteen hundred to sixteen hundred UTC for deterministic calculations and fair allocation. This is the natural fit for most real-world investments where you can't just dump unlimited capital in.  Term Halos use NFATs — Non-Fungible Allocation Tokens. These represent individual bespoke deals with negotiated terms within defined buyboxes, meaning acceptable parameter ranges. Each position can have different duration, size, and yield. Think six-month, twelve-month, and eighteen-month positions with different yields, all under the same legal framework. NFATs are transferable, can be used as collateral, and can even be wrapped in fungible ERC-20 tokens for secondary markets.  Trading Halos run AMM smart contracts using predeposited USDS to provide instant liquidity for RWA tokens. Users sell tokenized real-world assets for instant USDS at a spread, and the Trading Halo redeems with the underlying issuer over the normal settlement cycle. This turns real-world asset liquidity from days-long redemption into instant settlement.  There are also Special Halos for identity networks handling KYC, Exchange Halos running orderbooks and matching engines, and Staking Halos for Growth Staking. Plus Core Halos that wrap legacy positions like Morpho vaults, Aave pools, and SparkLend under the same risk framework.  Speaking of Growth Staking — this is a clever incentive mechanism.
