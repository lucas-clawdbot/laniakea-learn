This is a new system just documented in the spark-savings-intents repository that solves a real operational problem. When a user wants to withdraw a very large amount from a Spark Savings Vault — think millions of USDC — the vault might not have enough idle liquidity sitting around to process the redemption immediately. A direct on-chain redeem call would just revert, leaving them stuck.  The solution is an intent-based withdrawal mechanism. Instead of calling redeem directly, users submit a withdrawal intent specifying how many shares they want to redeem, who should receive the assets, and a deadline. The ALM Planner — an off-chain relayer system — monitors these intents, orchestrates the necessary liquidity by unwinding positions and moving assets into the vault, and then calls fulfill to atomically complete the redemption. The intent contract never holds any shares or assets itself. It's a pure coordination layer.  Three actors: the user who holds vault shares, the SavingsVaultIntents contract that stores requests and executes fulfillments, and the ALM Planner relayer that monitors events and orchestrates capital. A user can have one active request per vault at a time, can overwrite or cancel requests, and fulfillment is all-or-nothing — no partial fills. There's a clever race condition protection: the fulfill function takes a requestId parameter and validates it matches the stored request, preventing stale planner state from executing against a modified request.  Security posture is solid. Even a malicious relayer can't cause fund loss — worst they can do is not fulfill or let requests expire. There's a minimum intent size threshold to prevent spam. And the contract is designed specifically for Spark Vault v2. This is the plumbing that makes large institutional withdrawals work cleanly in the Laniakea context.
