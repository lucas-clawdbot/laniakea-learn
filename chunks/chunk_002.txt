SKY stakers must also hold growth assets like Agent tokens, risk capital tokens, or sSGA to unlock staking rewards. The more directly an asset contributes to innovation, the less you need. Agent tokens like Prime tokens need just 0.4x the value of your staked SKY. Junior risk capital needs 0.6x. Senior risk capital needs 1.0x. And passive sSGA needs 2.0x. This creates a powerful pull: same capital invested in a Prime token gives you five times the staking factor efficiency compared to holding sSGA. It structurally pushes capital from passive savings into the active innovation layer, and it eliminates mercenary staking because holding SKY alone earns you nothing.  Now the risk framework. This is genuinely sophisticated — it's inspired by Basel III banking regulation but adapted for decentralized operation. The core insight is that capital requirements should reflect the maximum loss that could actually be forced to realize, not just mark-to-market volatility.  There's a two-tier capital structure. Junior Risk Capital, or JRC, absorbs losses first in exchange for higher returns. Senior Risk Capital is protected by the JRC buffer and gets lower but safer returns. Each Prime provides its own JRC from its treasury — direct skin in the game.  The loss absorption waterfall is deep. At the Prime level: first, the Prime's own capital absorbs the first ten percent of JRC losses. Then all junior capital shares in. Then the Prime's governance token gets diluted through inflation — and this can cover losses essentially to infinity before touching senior capital. At the system level: senior risk capital absorbs next. Then SKY gets diluted. Nuclear options include genesis capital haircuts and USDS peg adjustment, which should never be reached.  Duration matching is where it gets really clever. Sky tracks how long USDS holders keep their positions using a Lindy model — the longer you've held, the longer you're likely to keep holding. This gives you liability duration data. Assets are then matched using Duration Buckets: if an asset takes a long time to liquidate, measured by something called Stressed Pull-to-Par time, it needs backing from correspondingly long-duration liabilities. Assets whose duration matches available liability capacity only need capital for fundamental risks like credit default. Unmatched assets need additional capital covering potential mark-to-market losses from credit spread widening. And all fixed-rate exposure must be hedged or carry extra capital.  There's also a risk capital ingression system — not all external capital counts at face value. It's discounted based on whether the provider is synomic, how long capital is committed, and the Prime's existing composition. A Prime token market cap constraint further limits total leverage. And concentration limits prevent any single correlated risk type from getting too large — anything exceeding a governance-defined category cap requires one hundred percent capitalization.  Let's talk about the smart contract architecture. Every layer uses the same building block: the PAU — Parallelized Allocation Unit. Three components: a Controller as the entry point enforcing rate limits, an ALMProxy that holds custody of funds and executes calls, and RateLimits for linear replenishment rate limits with configurable maximums and slopes. Same contracts everywhere — Generator, Prime, Halo, Foreign — just different configuration. This means no new code for new layers, factories can stamp out identical PAUs, and adding a layer just means deploying more instances.  There are four layers. The Generator layer interfaces with the stablecoin contract and deploys capital via ERC-4626 vaults. The Prime layer receives from Generators and deploys to Halos or bridges to other chains.
